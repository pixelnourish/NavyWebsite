Catch On! is a mobile game to teach kids about sustainable fishing

Most people who fish in Victoria are seasoned anglers. But Fisheries Victoria wanted to engage with a younger demographic. They asked us to visualise an iPad game that could help them do it.

"Catch On!" was our concept for a game that's fun, educational and uniquely Victorian.

Project summary

 
Audience Kids between the ages of 5 and 14
 
Technology iPad

What we did

We created a vision for a new product that was used to secure further government funding.

The result

A strategic direction for the game that was universally approved by stakeholders.

State Government Victoria

Our strategy:
connect the game world to reality

Getting kids to play a game is easy. But the goal of this project was to affect behaviour in the real world.

Our strategy was based on creating a clear link between the game world and the natural environment. Once kids could make this connection, it would be easy for them to take their knowledge offline.

Design theory

Exploring early concepts

We always start on paper. Sketches let us explore ideas quickly and cheaply before we move on to refined prototypes and designs.

Illustrating the Golden Perch
Our design direction blended fun, friendly illustration with real world imagery.

The Golden Perch is a fish that occurs naturally in Victorian tributaries of the Murray-Darling system. It can grow to 75 cm in length and 23 kg in weight, but is commonly less than 5 kg.

Using the realistic image as inspiration, we enhanced the natural colours of the fish, gave it a larger eye for added cuteness, and softened some of the scary looking fins on it's back.

In the marine discovery centre

Part of our strategy was to invest in a concept that would work on the iPad as well as it would on kiosks in a public space.

Creating good habits for sustainable fishing

 

The Marine and Freshwater Discovery Centre is a place where kids can learn about Victorian fisheries, recreational fishing and the marine and freshwater environment.

Throwing back fish that are too small is one of the keys to sustainable fishing. Kids are encouraged to measure fish themselves and make the right decision.
